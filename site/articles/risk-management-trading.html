Risk Management in Trading: A Practical Guide

By MI Traders · Trading Education

Most new traders focus almost entirely on finding the "right" entry point. Experienced traders know that how much you risk on each trade matters far more than being right every time. This is the core idea behind risk management, and it's what MI Trade Master's practice mode is built to teach through repetition, not theory.

Why Risk Management Comes First

A trader who wins 40% of their trades can still be profitable if they manage risk well. A trader who wins 70% of their trades can still lose their account if a single bad trade wipes out weeks of gains. Risk management is the difference between a bad trade and a account-ending trade.

Position Sizing

The 1-2% Rule

Many traders risk no more than 1-2% of their total account on any single trade. This means a string of losses — which happens to everyone — doesn't put the whole account in danger.

Position size should be calculated backward from your stop-loss distance and your risk percentage, not decided by "gut feeling" about how big a trade feels right.

Stop-Loss: Deciding Your Exit Before You Enter

A stop-loss is a predetermined price where you exit a losing trade automatically. Setting it before entering a trade — not while emotions are running high mid-trade — is one of the simplest habits that separates disciplined traders from impulsive ones.

Risk-Reward Ratio

Before entering a trade, compare how much you stand to lose against how much you stand to gain. A common target is a risk-reward ratio of at least 1:2, meaning the potential reward is twice the potential risk. This way, even a trader who is right less than half the time can still come out ahead.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Moving your stop-loss further away after a trade goes against you, hoping it reverses
  • Risking a much larger percentage after a loss to "win it back" quickly
  • Entering trades without knowing the exit point in advance
  • Ignoring position size and trading the same size regardless of setup quality

Practice Before You Risk Real Capital

Risk management is a skill built through repetition, not something you can memorize and apply perfectly on the first try. Practicing in a risk-free environment lets these habits become automatic before real money is on the line.
