Candlestick Patterns Guide for Beginners

By MI Traders · Trading Education

Candlestick charts are the most widely used way to read price action in trading. Every candle tells a short story about buyers and sellers fighting for control over a period of time. Once you learn to read that story, chart analysis stops feeling random. This guide from MI Trade Master covers the patterns beginners ask about the most.

What a Candlestick Shows

Each candle has a body and wicks (shadows). The body shows the range between the opening and closing price, while the wicks show the highest and lowest price reached in that period. A green or hollow body usually means price closed higher than it opened; a red or filled body means it closed lower.

Common Bullish Patterns

Hammer

A small body near the top of the candle with a long lower wick. It often appears after a downtrend and can signal buyers stepping in to push price back up.

Bullish Engulfing

A small red candle followed by a larger green candle that completely covers it. This shift often marks a change from selling pressure to buying pressure.

Morning Star

A three-candle pattern — a long red candle, a small indecisive candle, then a strong green candle — often seen at the bottom of a downtrend.

Common Bearish Patterns

Shooting Star

The opposite of a hammer — a small body near the bottom with a long upper wick, often appearing after an uptrend as a warning sign.

Bearish Engulfing

A small green candle followed by a larger red candle that swallows it completely, suggesting sellers have taken control.

Evening Star

A three-candle reversal pattern that mirrors the morning star but appears at the top of an uptrend, hinting at a possible drop.

The Doji — Indecision on the Chart

A Doji forms when the open and close are almost equal, leaving a tiny body with wicks on either side. It represents a tug-of-war where neither buyers nor sellers won. On its own it's not a strong signal, but combined with the candles around it, a Doji can hint that a trend is losing steam.

Patterns Are Context, Not Guarantees

No single candlestick pattern predicts the future with certainty. They work best as one piece of a bigger picture — alongside support and resistance levels, volume, and overall trend direction. Treat every pattern as a probability, not a promise.
